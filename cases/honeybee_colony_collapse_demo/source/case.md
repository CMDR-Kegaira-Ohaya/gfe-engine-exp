# Honeybee colony collapse disorder (demo case)

A managed honey bee colony can look provisionally intact and still move toward collapse. In the syndrome called **colony collapse disorder (CCD)**, the striking pattern is the disappearance of most adult worker bees while the queen, brood, and food stores remain behind for at least a time. Few or no dead adult bees are found in or near the hive, and robbing by neighboring colonies is often delayed rather than immediate. [HB-01] [HB-02] [HB-03]

This demo case is rebuilt from an older v12.7 case idea, but the factual baseline here is re-established from fresh sources rather than inherited blindly from the older note. The point is not to prove that CCD has a single accepted cause. The point is to preserve a well-documented pattern and then ask whether 15.7 makes more of that pattern structurally legible.

## Source baseline

The cited baseline is deliberately modest:

- CCD refers to a colony pattern in which the majority of worker bees disappear, leaving a queen, food, and some immature bees behind. [HB-01] [HB-02]
- A hallmark of CCD reports is the lack of dead adult worker bees in or around the hive. [HB-02] [HB-03]
- Delayed robbing and delayed invasion by hive pests were part of the descriptive symptom cluster used in early CCD work. [HB-03]
- The best-supported official and research summaries treat CCD as a **multi-stressor problem**, not as a settled one-cause event. Parasites, pathogens, nutrition, genetics, management stress, and pesticide exposure all appear in the causal field. [HB-04] [HB-05]

## Focal questions

- What actually collapses first when the hive still contains a queen, brood, and stored food?
- How should the case distinguish a sudden poisoning event from an accumulated multi-stressor collapse pattern?
- What becomes more visible when worker disappearance, brood persistence, food persistence, field pressure, and delayed robbing are kept distinct instead of being flattened into one generic die-pff?

## Reading orientation

Read the case on at least two levels at once.

1. **Local colony level** — worker force, queen and brood, stored reserves, and the colony's internal coordination.
2. **Field level** — mites, pathogens, pesticide exposure, forage quality, transport stress, weather, and other outside pressures that may accumulate before the colony presents the final pattern. [HB-04] [HB-05]

Do not overclaim from the source record. The factual baseline here is the descriptive pattern plus the multi-stressor field. Stronger structural language in the reading is interpretation layered on top of that source base, not a replacement for it.
